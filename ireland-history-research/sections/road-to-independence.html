7. The Road to Independence

The late 19th and early 20th centuries were dominated by the campaign for Home Rule, or self-government for Ireland. The Irish Parliamentary Party, led by Charles Stewart Parnell, made significant gains, but Home Rule bills were repeatedly defeated or postponed [1].

The Easter Rising

Frustration with the political process led a small group of Irish republicans to stage the Easter Rising in Dublin in 1916. The rebellion was quickly crushed, and initially, it had little public support. However, the British decision to execute 15 of its leaders provoked widespread public outrage and a massive swing in public opinion in favor of the rebels and the cause of independence [2].

The War of Independence and Partition

In the 1918 general election, the republican party Sinn Féin won a landslide victory. Their elected members refused to sit in the British Parliament, instead forming their own Irish parliament, the Dáil Éireann, and declaring independence [1].

This led to the Irish War of Independence (1919-1921), a guerrilla war between the Irish Republican Army (IRA) and British forces, including the controversial Black and Tans. The war ended with the Anglo-Irish Treaty in 1921. The treaty created the Irish Free State, consisting of 26 of Ireland's 32 counties, as a self-governing dominion within the British Empire. The remaining six counties in the north, which had a Protestant majority, remained part of the United Kingdom, creating Northern Ireland. This partition of the island would become a central issue for the next century [1].

From Failure to Success: The Easter Rising was a military failure but a political masterpiece, transforming public opinion and paving the way for the War of Independence and the eventual creation of the Irish state.
