The relationship between Iran and Israel is one of the most volatile and complex geopolitical dynamics in the modern Middle East. Once allies, they are now archenemies locked in a multi-front conflict that spans covert operations, proxy wars, and direct military confrontation. This deep-seated animosity is not just a bilateral issue; it involves a web of regional actors and has significant global implications, with the United States playing a crucial, and often controversial, role.

This article provides an objective overview of the Iran-Israel conflict, tracing its roots from a strategic partnership to a bitter rivalry. We will explore the historical context, including the pivotal 1979 Iranian Revolution, the influence of Iran's 'Axis of Resistance,' the critical role of the United States, and the ever-present threat of Iran's nuclear program. By examining these key facets, we aim to provide a comprehensive understanding of how this conflict developed and where it might be heading.

1. Introduction

The story of Iran and Israel is a dramatic tale of transformation. For decades, they were partners, sharing strategic interests in a turbulent Middle East. Iran under the Pahlavi dynasty was the second Muslim-majority nation to recognize Israel, and the two countries built a relationship based on trade, military cooperation, and a shared suspicion of Arab nationalism [1]. However, the 1979 Iranian Revolution shattered this alliance, replacing it with an ideological animosity that has defined their interactions ever since. The new Islamic Republic, led by Ayatollah Khomeini, branded Israel the "Little Satan"â€”an illegitimate entity in the heart of the Muslim worldâ€”and pledged its support to the Palestinian cause [2].

Today, their conflict is a "shadow war" fought across the region, from the battlefields of Syria to the shipping lanes of the Red Sea. It involves sophisticated cyber-attacks, covert assassinations of nuclear scientists, and the arming of powerful proxy groups. This article delves into the history and key dimensions of this conflict, providing a factual and objective overview of how two former allies became locked in a dangerous and seemingly intractable struggle.

2. From Allies to Adversaries: A Historical Overview

The Pahlavi Era: A Strategic Alliance

Before 1979, the relationship between Iran and Israel was defined by strategic cooperation. Under the rule of Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, Iran became the second Muslim-majority country to recognize Israel's sovereignty in 1950 [1]. This was not a sentimental alliance, but a pragmatic one. Israel, surrounded by hostile Arab states, pursued a "periphery doctrine," seeking alliances with non-Arab powers in the region. Iran, under the Western-backed Shah, was a natural fit. The two countries engaged in extensive trade, with Iran becoming a key supplier of oil to Israel. They also shared intelligence and cooperated on military projects, viewing each other as a bulwark against the influence of Arab nationalist movements and the Soviet Union [2].

The 1979 Revolution: A Turning Point

The 1979 Islamic Revolution was the cataclysm that shattered this alliance. The overthrow of the Shah and the rise of Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini created a new political and ideological order in Iran. The new regime was vehemently anti-Western and, by extension, anti-Israel. All formal ties were severed, the Israeli embassy in Tehran was handed over to the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO), and Israel was recast as an illegitimate "Zionist entity" [1]. The revolution was not just a political shift; it was an ideological one that placed the Palestinian cause at the center of Iran's foreign policy, reframing it as a pan-Islamic struggle against oppression [2].

The "Little Satan" and the "Great Satan": The New Ideological Landscape

In the post-revolutionary landscape, Ayatollah Khomeini famously branded the United States the "Great Satan" and Israel the "Little Satan." This rhetoric was more than just political posturing; it was a core component of the new regime's identity and worldview. Israel was seen as an imperialist outpost of the United States in the Middle East, and its existence was considered an injustice against the Palestinian people and the entire Muslim world. This ideological framework has guided Iran's policies for over four decades, leading to its support for anti-Israel militant groups and its unwavering opposition to any peace process that recognizes Israel's right to exist [9].

3. The American Role: A Complicated History

The 1953 Coup: The "Original Sin"

It is impossible to understand the Iran-U.S. relationshipâ€”and, by extension, Iran's posture towards Israelâ€”without acknowledging the 1953 coup. In a covert operation, the CIA and British intelligence orchestrated the overthrow of Iran's democratically elected Prime Minister, Mohammad Mossadegh, who had nationalized the country's oil industry. The coup reinstalled the pro-Western Shah, but it left a deep and lasting scar on the Iranian psyche. For many Iranians, this event is the "original sin" of American interference, a betrayal that proved the U.S. was not a partner but a neo-colonial power. This historical grievance has fueled decades of anti-American sentiment and is a foundational element of the Islamic Republic's ideology [8].

U.S. Alliance with the Shah and Israel

Following the coup, the United States became the Shah's primary international patron, providing him with extensive military and economic aid. This solidified Iran's position as a key "pillar" of U.S. security policy in the Persian Gulf during the Cold War. At the same time, the U.S. maintained its "unshakeable" alliance with Israel. This created a triangular relationship where the U.S. was allied with two regional partners who were, in turn, allied with each other. However, the U.S. support for the Shah's increasingly autocratic and repressive regime, including the notorious SAVAK secret police, further alienated the Iranian population and linked the U.S. to the Shah's eventual downfall [9].

Post-1979: Sanctions, the JCPOA, and "Maximum Pressure"

After the 1979 revolution and the Iran hostage crisis, the U.S.-Iran relationship devolved into one of open hostility. The U.S. has since pursued a policy of containment and pressure, primarily through economic sanctions, aimed at curbing Iran's nuclear ambitions and its support for regional proxies. The most significant diplomatic effort to resolve the nuclear issue was the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA), or the Iran nuclear deal, which lifted sanctions in exchange for verifiable limits on Iran's nuclear program. However, the U.S. withdrew from the deal in 2018 under the Trump administration, launching a "maximum pressure" campaign of renewed sanctions. This move, while praised by Israel, led Iran to accelerate its nuclear activities and has brought the region to the brink of a wider conflict [10].

4. Iran's 'Axis of Resistance': A Network of Proxies

A central pillar of Iran's foreign policy is its cultivation of the "Axis of Resistance," a network of state and non-state actors across the Middle East that allows it to project power and challenge its adversaries, particularly Israel and the United States. This network is not a monolithic bloc, but a collection of strategic partnerships, with each group possessing its own local agenda and degree of autonomy [5].

Hezbollah: The Pivotal Partner

Hezbollah, the powerful Shiite political and militant group in Lebanon, is Iran's most important and capable proxy. Founded with the direct assistance of Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) in 1982, Hezbollah has evolved into a formidable military force with a sophisticated arsenal of rockets and missiles, much of it supplied by Iran. The relationship is not merely transactional; it is deeply ideological, rooted in a shared commitment to resisting Israeli and U.S. influence in the region. Iran provides Hezbollah with an estimated $700 million to $1 billion annually, making it the cornerstone of the Axis of Resistance and a significant threat on Israel's northern border [6].

Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad: A Marriage of Convenience

Iran's support extends to Sunni Palestinian groups like Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ). This alliance is often described as a "marriage of convenience," as it bridges the Sunni-Shia divide based on a shared enemy. Iran provides these groups with crucial funding, weapons, and training, significantly enhancing their ability to challenge Israel from Gaza. While this relationship has faced strains, particularly over the civil war in Syria, the practical need for a state sponsor has kept these groups within Iran's orbit. Iran's support for Hamas is estimated to be around $70-100 million per year, a critical lifeline for its military wing [4][7].

The Role of These Groups in the Conflict with Israel

These proxy groups are the primary instruments through which Iran wages its long-running conflict with Israel. They allow Iran to maintain a strategy of "forward defense," confronting Israel on its borders without engaging in direct state-to-state warfare. Hezbollah's massive rocket arsenal poses a constant threat to northern Israel, while Hamas and PIJ can launch attacks from Gaza, creating a multi-front challenge for Israeli defense planners. This proxy strategy allows Iran to bleed Israel's resources, maintain pressure, and deter a direct attack on its own soil, all while maintaining a degree of plausible deniability [5].

5. The Shadow War and Direct Confrontation

Covert Operations and Assassinations

For years, the Iran-Israel conflict was largely a "shadow war," fought through covert operations, sabotage, and assassinations. Israel is widely believed to be behind a series of attacks targeting Iran's nuclear program, including the assassination of several top Iranian nuclear scientists and the deployment of sophisticated cyberweapons like the Stuxnet virus, which caused significant damage to Iran's uranium enrichment centrifuges [2]. Iran, in turn, has been accused of plotting attacks against Israeli and Jewish targets worldwide. This clandestine warfare has created a cycle of retaliation, raising tensions and increasing the risk of miscalculation.

The Conflict in Syria

The Syrian civil war transformed the landscape of the Iran-Israel conflict. Iran intervened heavily to support the regime of Bashar al-Assad, establishing a significant military presence in the country. This provided Iran with a new front from which to threaten Israel and a land bridge to supply its proxy, Hezbollah, in Lebanon. In response, Israel has conducted hundreds of airstrikes in Syria, targeting Iranian military installations, weapons convoys, and personnel. This has turned Syria into a major theater of direct, albeit undeclared, confrontation between the two adversaries.

Recent Escalations and Direct Attacks

In recent years, the shadow war has spilled out into the open. A series of attacks on Israeli- and Iranian-linked commercial vessels in the Persian Gulf and the Red Sea have demonstrated a new willingness to engage in direct military action. The most significant escalation occurred in April 2024, when Iran launched its first-ever direct missile and drone attack on Israeli territory, in retaliation for a suspected Israeli airstrike on an Iranian consular building in Damascus. While the attack was largely intercepted, it shattered the long-standing taboo against direct state-to-state conflict and brought the two nations to the precipice of a full-scale regional war [3].

6. The Nuclear Issue: The Ultimate Red Line

A Brief History of Iran's Nuclear Program

Ironically, Iran's nuclear program was initiated with U.S. assistance in the 1950s under the "Atoms for Peace" program. After the 1979 revolution, the program was secretly revived and expanded. For decades, Iran has insisted its nuclear activities are for peaceful energy purposes, but its clandestine activities and lack of transparency have led the international community to suspect it is pursuing nuclear weapons capabilities. This has been the central point of contention between Iran and the West for over two decades [8].

Israel's Stance and Actions

For Israel, a nuclear-armed Iran is an existential threat. Israeli leaders have repeatedly stated that they will never allow Iran to acquire a nuclear weapon. This has translated into a policy of preemption and sabotage. As mentioned, Israel is widely believed to be responsible for numerous covert attacks aimed at crippling Iran's nuclear program. Israeli officials have also repeatedly threatened to take unilateral military action to destroy Iran's nuclear facilities if diplomatic efforts fail, viewing it as a last resort to prevent a "nightmare scenario" [3].

The International Dimension and the JCPOA

The international community, led by the United States and other world powers, has long sought to resolve the Iranian nuclear issue through a combination of sanctions and diplomacy. This culminated in the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA), which placed significant and verifiable restrictions on Iran's nuclear program in exchange for sanctions relief. While supporters hailed it as a landmark nonproliferation agreement, critics, including Israel, argued it was flawed and did not go far enough. The U.S. withdrawal from the deal in 2018 and the subsequent re-imposition of sanctions have left the agreement in tatters and pushed Iran to advance its enrichment activities to near-weapons-grade levels, making the situation more dangerous than ever [10].

7. Conclusion

The conflict between Iran and Israel is a deeply entrenched, multi-faceted rivalry with a long and complex history. From a pragmatic alliance of convenience, the relationship has curdled into an ideological and geopolitical struggle that threatens to engulf the entire Middle East. The 1979 Iranian Revolution, the enduring role of the United States, the proxy warfare of the "Axis of Resistance," and the existential stakes of the nuclear program are all critical threads in this tangled web.

Today, the conflict is more dangerous and unpredictable than ever. The "shadow war" has given way to direct military confrontation, and the collapse of the JCPOA has left Iran's nuclear program largely unconstrained. There are no easy solutions, and the path forward is fraught with risk. Understanding the historical grievances, strategic calculations, and ideological motivations of all parties involved is the first step toward comprehending the magnitude of the challenge and the potential consequences of further escalation. The future of the Middle East may well depend on whether these two powerful adversaries can find a way to step back from the brink.
