1. Introduction: The Language Barrier in Science

The global scientific community faces a profound language accessibility challenge. Despite scientific research being inherently international, the vast majority of academic journals publish exclusively in English, creating significant barriers for non-native English speakers who represent over 93% of the world's population.

The Scale of the Problem: Around 98% of all peer-reviewed scientific articles are published in English, but only around 7% of the world's population speaks English as a first language.

This linguistic dominance creates challenges across multiple aspects of science, from biases in peer review against non-native English writers to global implications for science-informed policy. The availability of scientific literature in a person's native language could play a crucial role in shaping their decision to pursue a career in science.

The Promise of LLM-Powered Translation

Recent advances in LLMs offer unprecedented opportunities for scientific translation. Unlike traditional NMT systems, LLMs can process complex instructions and generate context-aware, customized outputs tailored to scientific community requirements.

  • Match and often surpass NMT systems in translation performance
  • Handle domain-specific vocabulary and formatting preferences
  • Incorporate feedback from researchers through in-context learning
  • Adapt to specialized requirements of scientific communities

Key Contributions

This research makes three major contributions:

2. Journal-Compatible Translation

For multilingual translation to be widely adopted in scientific publishing, the process must be practical for journals to implement. This research demonstrates how LLMs can preserve the complex formatting of journal articles during translation, offering an approach that is both adaptable and easy to integrate.

The JATS XML Standard

Since 2002, the JATS XML protocol has become the global standard for academic publishing. Despite different publishers having distinct visual styles, they all share the same underlying JATS structure with universal elements like <front>, <body>, <abstract>, and <contrib-group> sections.

Technical Achievement: GPT-4o successfully translates article text while preserving surrounding XML tags, maintaining complex structures including figures, tables, equations, and nested elements with 99.7% accuracy.

Implementation Strategy

  • Process articles in memory-manageable chunks (typically 5 paragraphs)
  • Prepend prompts with full original document content for context
  • Translate tables and figures independently to ensure structural accuracy
  • Include specific instructions to prevent text truncation

This method successfully translated full articles into 28 different languages while fully preserving JATS formatting, making it applicable at the final stage of publication or to already-published articles. The approach ensures maximum compatibility with existing publication frameworks and paves the way for widespread translation adoption across different journals.

3. QA-Style Automated Benchmarking

Traditional machine translation evaluation relies on automated metrics like BLEU, which require parallel reference datasets. Since no such dataset exists for scientific translations across diverse languages and disciplines, this research introduces a novel question-and-answer benchmarking method.

Innovation: The QA method automatically generates comprehension questions from the original article, then tests whether the translated version conveys the same information - no parallel datasets required.

Benchmarking Procedure

  1. LLM generates 50 multiple-choice questions capturing key details from the original English article
  2. Same LLM reads the translated article and attempts to answer these questions
  3. Performance accuracy indicates how well the translation conveys scientific details
  4. Safeguards prevent the model from using prior knowledge instead of the translated text

Remarkable Results

Testing across six articles spanning cardiology, archaeology, neurology, quantum optics, drug delivery, and materials science, the method achieved impressive performance across 28 languages:

Overall Performance: 95.9% average accuracy

  • Highest: Swedish (98.0%)
  • Lowest: Tamil (91.7%)
  • Range: No individual score below 84%
  • Success: 23 languages achieved 100% on at least one article

The high QA results indicate that the translation approach effectively conveys key findings and essential details across diverse scientific disciplines. Even "low-resource" languages like Urdu, Telugu, and Tamil performed well, demonstrating the method's broad applicability.

Validation: Comparisons with plain text translations by GPT-4o (96.0%) and Google Translate (95.8%) showed no degradation from preserving XML structure, confirming that structured content translates as effectively as unstructured text.

4. Feedback from Authors

To complement the automated QA benchmarking, researchers gathered feedback from 15 scientists across various disciplines including quantum optics, nanophotonics, biostatistics, materials science, and machine learning. Each participant reviewed a translation of their own scientific paper in their native language.

Overall Translation Quality

The user study reinforced the QA benchmarking findings: nearly all researchers (93.3%) reported that translations contained few or no issues in conveying original information. Most participants (86.7%) agreed that other scientists would understand the key ideas from the translations.

The Overtranslation Challenge

A key insight emerged regarding technical vocabulary handling. Many participants (86.7%) expressed dissatisfaction with how overtranslation was handled, identifying two main types of issues:

Technical terms exist in the native language but were translated awkwardly or incorrectly (e.g., "edge coupling" ‚Üí French "couplage par bord" instead of the preferred "couplage par la tranche")

üîÑ Overtranslation

Technical terms don't exist or are rarely used in the native language, and the original English is preferred (e.g., "rigorous coupled-wave analysis" kept in English rather than literal Korean translation)

Cultural Linguistic Patterns: Speakers of languages with more English loanwords (like Korean) tend to favor English technical terms compared to those from languages with fewer loanwords (like French).

5. Feedback-Adaptive Translation

Responding to the overtranslation concerns identified in the user study, the researchers developed a targeted approach using in-context learning to preserve selected English technical terms during translation.

One-Shot Prompting Strategy

The challenge of deciding which terms to keep and which to translate is inherently subjective. The solution employs one-shot prompting, providing GPT-4o with a verified example translation where an author has specifically identified terms that should remain in English.

Example Process: A scientific excerpt is translated with specific technical terms (like "transition metal dichalcogenides", "direct bandgap") preserved in English based on author preference. This curated example then guides translations of other texts.

Diverse User Preferences

Follow-up surveys with five authors revealed varied preferences when comparing direct translations versus English-term-preserving versions:

  • 3 of 5 participants preferred retaining some English terms for more natural scientific text
  • 2 of 5 participants favored complete translation over English terms
  • Language patterns emerged: Speakers of languages with more English loanwords (Korean) favored English terms vs. those with fewer loanwords (French)
  • Hybrid approach suggested: Present English terms in brackets alongside translations

Flexibility Advantage: The strength of LLM-based translation lies in its ability to integrate diverse customization and feedback, enabling tailored translations that can be adapted to various vocabulary and stylistic preferences through additional examples.

6. Discussion and Implications

This research demonstrates that LLM-powered translation can successfully bridge language barriers in scientific publishing through tailored approaches that consider both publishers and authors. The method translates scientific articles while maintaining JATS XML structure, opening new possibilities for academic journals to include multilingual translations.

While the approach shows great promise, several areas warrant further investigation:

  • Term consistency: Translating articles in sections can lead to vocabulary variations between API calls
  • Context awareness: Enhancing scientific translation context may reduce inconsistencies
  • Model comparison: Testing other LLMs beyond GPT-4o for XML handling capabilities
  • Scale expansion: Larger surveys could reveal additional translation preference patterns

Future Impact: If adopted for large-scale scientific translation, the resulting corpora could be used to fine-tune models, further improving consistency and overall translation quality while fostering broader accessibility and collaboration in global research.

üìö Glossary

JATS XML

Journal Article Tag Suite - XML protocol for structuring scientific journal articles, now the global standard for academic publishing

LLM

Large Language Models - Advanced AI systems capable of understanding and generating human-like text based on vast training data

NMT

Neural Machine Translation - AI systems specifically designed for translating between languages using neural networks

QA Benchmarking

Question-and-Answer evaluation method that tests comprehension of translated content without requiring parallel datasets

BLEU

Bilingual Evaluation Understudy - A metric for evaluating machine translation by comparing against reference translations

In-Context Learning

Few-shot prompting technique where models learn from curated examples to improve performance on similar tasks

Overtranslation

When technical terms are translated literally into the target language when keeping the English term would be preferred by domain experts

One-Shot Prompting

A machine learning technique where a model is given one example to learn from before performing a task

Code-Switching

Mixing languages in translation, keeping some terminology in the original language while translating the rest
