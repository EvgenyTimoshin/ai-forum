8. Modern Ireland: A Tale of Two States

The Anglo-Irish Treaty split the republican movement, leading to the bitter Irish Civil War (1922-1923) between the pro-Treaty forces, who supported the new Irish Free State, and the anti-Treaty IRA, who wanted a full republic. The pro-Treaty side was victorious, but the conflict left a lasting legacy on Irish politics [1].

The Republic of Ireland

The Irish Free State gradually distanced itself from Britain. In 1937, a new constitution was adopted, and the state was renamed Ireland (or Ã‰ire). It remained neutral during World War II. In 1949, Ireland formally declared itself a republic and left the British Commonwealth [2].

The latter half of the 20th century saw significant economic and social change. After decades of economic stagnation and high emigration, Ireland experienced a period of rapid economic growth from the 1990s onwards, which became known as the Celtic Tiger. Socially, the country became more liberal, with the influence of the Catholic Church declining [2].

Northern Ireland and The Troubles

In Northern Ireland, the 20th century was marked by sectarian conflict. The Catholic minority faced discrimination in housing, jobs, and political power from the ruling Protestant Unionist majority. This led to a civil rights movement in the late 1960s, which was met with a violent response [1].

This period, known as The Troubles, lasted for three decades and involved paramilitary groups like the IRA and loyalist forces, as well as the British Army. The conflict resulted in over 3,000 deaths. A peace process eventually led to the Good Friday Agreement in 1998, which established a power-sharing government in Northern Ireland [1].

A Hard-Won Peace: The Good Friday Agreement was a landmark achievement in the peace process, creating a framework for a devolved, power-sharing government and largely bringing an end to decades of violence in Northern Ireland.
